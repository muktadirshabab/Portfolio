    About Me

    My Journey in Art

    In the shadow of losing my parents, I found a flicker of life in art. 5th grade brought desperation, failed grades, and cruel laughs—but with stubborn hands, I drew my first comic characters. Each stroke felt like stepping into another world, and without knowing, I had fallen in love with art. The road was lonely, home offered no support, and harsh words followed me—but my aunt’s unwavering faith became my secret strength.

    I still remember the pain of being beaten for drawing while I tried to study, the heavy conditions placed on me just to score above 90. From fifth grade to higher secondary, I clung to the front row, desperate to hold onto my art, yet the world offered no support. Then, like a quiet light in the darkness, my teacher Roni Kumar Nath stepped in. With his belief in me, I discovered a new path—a way to nurture both my studies and my art, and finally, hope began to take root.

    Amid the struggles, a quiet love bloomed in my life. She lifted me when I stumbled, reminded me to stay human, to keep moving. Without realizing, I fell for her—pure, unconscious, inevitable. She is my unwritten story: Umme Kulum Arifa. Yet, a shadow of doubt lingers—will her love remain, will she truly hold my hand forever? Still, in the trembling uncertainty, hope flickers, and I hold on to every fleeting moment with her.

    With my aunt’s support, I studied formally at Chittagong Shilpakala Academy, where Sadia Ma’am showed me new horizons. I’ve walked barefoot through streets, faced endless harassment, and been cheated—but I worked tirelessly, anywhere, anytime, for free. I’m not rich or famous yet, but I am a simple artist, writer, and filmmaker, fluent in stories even if budgets hold back my films. Teaching and creating are now my life. The system offers little support, so I push for change—and I ask you to join me in this fight, because giving up was never an option.

    Education

    Inspirations

    I’ve always been inspired by those wall painters—the ones who don’t get a proper education, who aren’t respected as artists, yet pour their hearts into every stroke. But the moment I stand in their shoes and try the work myself, I realize how challenging it really is. That’s when I understood something crucial: you don’t become an artist just by knowing how to draw. You have to carry art in your heart and pursue it through every circumstance.

    When you do that, art returns to you in its purest form. Respect or recognition doesn’t matter at first—what matters is the pursuit, the passion. I don’t draw just for the sake of drawing; I draw because I love art. And the more I love it, the more I practice, the more it grows inside me. In that dedication, the respect and admiration come naturally. That’s my journey, my promise to myself, and my call to anyone who dares to chase their passion relentlessly.
